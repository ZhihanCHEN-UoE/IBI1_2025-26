Working through Practical 9 on infection modelling was a highly engaging yet progressively challenging experience that significantly deepened my understanding of stochastic computational epidemiology. While implementing the basic SIR model felt straightforward due to its clear probabilistic logic and dictionary-based state tracking, extending it to incorporate varying vaccination rates and, later, a two-dimensional spatial grid introduced substantial complexity that required careful algorithmic planning and iterative debugging. The most intellectually rewarding aspect was observing how small adjustments to parameters or initial conditions dramatically altered epidemic trajectories, particularly when visualizing the herd immunity threshold across different vaccination percentages and watching the spatial heatmaps dynamically reveal how localized transmission clusters emerge, expand, and eventually burn out. One of the most critical technical hurdles I successfully navigated was ensuring synchronous state updates by operating on array copies within each time step, which prevented newly infected cells from immediately transmitting the disease during the same iteration and underscored the importance of precise temporal discretization in agent-based simulations. Ultimately, this practical not only strengthened my proficiency with NumPy array manipulation, boundary condition handling, and Matplotlib visualization, but also instilled a deeper appreciation for how computational frameworks can translate abstract biological mechanisms into tangible, visual insights, reinforcing the valuable lesson that even simplified stochastic models can powerfully capture the inherent unpredictability and spatial dependencies of real-world disease dynamics.
